Curve 1 / goods market

The IS curve

The IS curve collects all combinations of the interest rate and income at which planned demand for goods exactly equals output. Its starting point is the goods-market equilibrium condition, filled in with the behavioural equations for consumption and investment:

Y = C + I + G
C = c₀ + c₁·(Y − T)   ·   I = I₀ − b·i
⇒  Y = 1/(1−c₁) · [ c₀ − c₁·T + I₀ − b·i + G ] Y real income (GDP) · i interest rate · C consumption · I investment · G government spending · T taxes · c₀ autonomous consumption · c₁ marginal propensity to consume · I₀ autonomous investment · b interest sensitivity of investment

The interest rate enters through one single term: −b·i. A higher interest rate makes credit more expensive, investment falls, and with it total demand and income. That is why the IS curve slopes downward.

Fiscal policy lives inside the bracket: raising G (or cutting T) increases demand at every interest rate — the whole curve shifts to the right.

Curve 2 / money market

The LM curve

The LM curve collects all combinations of the interest rate and income at which money demand equals the money supply set by the central bank. Solving the money-market equilibrium for i gives the curve directly:

M/P = L(i, Y) = k·Y − h·i
⇒  i = (k/h)·Y − (1/h)·(M/P) M money supply · P price level · L liquidity (money demand) · k income sensitivity of money demand (k>0) · h interest rate sensitivity of money demand (h>0)

Income enters through k·Y: more income means more transactions, so people want to hold more money. With a fixed money supply, the interest rate must rise to keep the market in balance — the LM curve slopes upward.

Monetary policy works through M/P: more money shifts LM to the right and lowers the interest rate. And the parameter h controls the slope — a very large h makes LM almost flat. Remember that; it is the key to Japan.

Synthesis / both markets at once

The joint equilibrium

The two curves describe different markets, but they share the same two variables. Neither equation can be solved alone: the IS equation contains the interest rate (through −b·i), and the LM equation contains income (through k·Y). The markets are linked in both directions:

Y↑ → k·Y↑ → i↑Higher income raises money demand; with fixed M/P the interest rate must rise (movement along LM).
i↑ → −b·i → Y↓A higher interest rate cuts investment, lowering demand and income (movement along IS).
⇒ one point (Y*, i*)Because each market reacts to the other, exactly one combination clears both at once: the intersection.

Mathematically, the equilibrium is found by inserting the LM equation into the IS equation and solving:

Y* = [ A/b + (M/P)/h ] ÷ [ (1−c₁)/b + k/h ]
i* = (k/h)·Y* − (1/h)·(M/P) with A = c₀ − c₁·T + I₀ + G (autonomous demand). The solution contains fiscal variables (G, T) and monetary variables (M/P): output and the interest rate are determined jointly by both markets. This is exactly the formula the model lab evaluates live.
One sentence to remember: the goods market determines how much is produced, the money market determines at which interest rate — and because each depends on the other, only (Y*, i*) makes everything consistent.

Case study / 1995 – 2022

Japan: stagnation despite record spending

Japan is the textbook stress test for IS-LM. After its asset bubble burst in the early 1990s, the government spent for decades — and the economy barely grew. The model can explain why, using nothing but the two curves above.

≈ 0.1 %
GDP growth in 2024 — effectively stagnation, with only ~0.6 % expected for 2025.
> 200 %
Government debt relative to GDP — the highest ratio of any industrialised country.
0 %
Interest rate on Japanese government bonds issued between 2016 and 2022.
≈ 0.5 %
The Bank of Japan's estimate of potential growth — the structural ceiling.

Step 1 — the liquidity trap. With the interest rate at zero, the LM curve becomes flat (in the model: h → very large). The interest rate cannot fall below zero, and extra money is simply held rather than lent. Monetary policy loses its grip — shifting LM no longer moves anything.

Step 2 — the fiscal puzzle. On a flat LM curve, fiscal policy should work especially well: no crowding-out, because i does not rise. The catch: while the state pushed IS to the right with high G, households and firms pushed it back to the left at the same time — deflation made waiting profitable, and the private sector was paying down bubble-era debt instead of investing (the "balance-sheet recession"). Government spending only offset the private collapse. Net effect: stagnation.

Step 3 — the model's limit. IS-LM is a short-run demand model. Japan's ceiling today is largely on the supply side: a shrinking, ageing workforce and weak productivity growth hold potential output near 0.5 % — something no demand curve can fix. Knowing where the model ends is part of understanding it.

See it yourself: the Japan preset sets h = 60 (flat LM) and weak investment sensitivity. Then move the M/P slider — almost nothing happens to Y*. Now move G — that still works. That asymmetry is the liquidity trap.
